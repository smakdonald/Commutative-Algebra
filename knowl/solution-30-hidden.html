To check this, we just need to see that each of the ideals \((x^2,xy,y^2)\) and \((x^2,y)\) are primary. Observe that each has radical \(\fm=(x,y)\text{,}\) which is maximal, so these ideals are both primary.[^1] In fact, our ideal \(I\) has infinitely many minimal primary decompositions: given any \(n \geqslant 1\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} I = (x) \cap (x^2, xy, y^n) \end{equation*}
is an irredundant primary decomposition. One thing all of these have in common is the radicals of the primary components: they are always \((x)\) and \((x,y)\text{.}\)
