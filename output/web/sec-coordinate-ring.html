    Section 4.2 The Coordinate Ring of a Variety

    “Drink whatever you like, just so long as it coordinates with your nail varnish.”
    ―Victoria Beckham

    Definition 4.18. Morphism of Varieties.

    Suppose \(X\) is a subvariety of \(\mathbb{A}^m_k\) and \(Y\) is a subvariety of \(\mathbb{A}^n_l\text{.}\) A morphism of varieties or algebraic map or regular map from \(X\) to \(Y\) is a function \(\phi: X \to Y\) defined coordinatewise by polynomials \(g_1, \dots, g_n\in k[x_1, \dots, x_m]\text{,}\) that is
    \begin{equation*} \phi(a_1, \dots, a_m) = (g_1(a_1, \dots, a_m), \dots, g_n(a_1, \dots, a_m)) \text{ for all } \bf{a} \in X. \end{equation*}
    A morphism of varieties \(\phi: X \to Y\) is an isomorphism if there is some morphism of varieties \(\phi: Y \to X\) such that \(\phi\circ \psi = \id_Y\) and \(\psi \circ \phi = \id_X\text{.}\)

    Example 4.19. .

    1. Let \(k\) be an infinite field. Consider \(X = \cZ(xy -1) \subseteq \A^2_k\) (i.e., \(X\) is a hyperbola) and define \(\phi: X \to \A^1_k\) by \(\phi(a,b) = a\text{.}\) Then \(\phi\) is an algebraic map (indeed, it is given by a linear polynomial) and its image is \(\A^1_k \setminus \{0\}\text{,}\) which is not an algebraic subset of \(\A^1_k\text{.}\) So the set-theoretic image of a morphism of varieties need not be a variety.
    2. Take an infinite field, and let \(Y\) be the classical cuspidal curve:
      \begin{equation*} Y = \cZ(y^2 - x^3) \subseteq \A^2_k. \end{equation*}
      Define
      \begin{equation*} \phi: \A^1_K \to Y \quad \phi(t) = (t^2, t^3). \end{equation*}
    This \(\phi\) is an algebraic map from \(\A^1_K\) to \(Y\text{,}\) since the component functions are polynomial functions of \(t\) and \((t^3)^2 - (t^2)^3 = 0\) for all \(t\text{.}\)
    Note that this \(\phi\) is a bijection of sets. However, it is not an isomorphism. Indeed, if it were, we would have some map \(\psi\) such that \(\psi\circ \phi = \mathrm{id}_{\A^1_K}\text{.}\) This \(\psi\) would be given by a polynomial \(h\) in two variables such that \(h(t^2,t^3) = t\text{.}\) It is easy to see that no such \(h\) exists. (c) Consider \(\A^6_k\) as the space of \(2 \times 3\) matrices over \(K\) with coordinates \(x_{11},\dots,x_{23}\text{,}\) and consider \(\A^5_K\) as the space of pairs of \(2 \times 1\) and \(1\times 3\) matrices over \(K\) with coordinates \(y_1,y_2, z_1,z_2,z_3\text{.}\) The map of matrix multiplication from \(\A^5_k\) to \(\A^6_k\) is a regular map:
    \begin{equation*} (y_1,y_2,z_1,z_2,z_3) \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} y_1 z_1 & y_1 z_2 & y_1 z_3 \\ y_2 z_1 & y_2 z_2 & y_2 z_3 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

    Definition 4.20. Coordinate Ring.

    Let \(k\) be a field, and \(X=\mathcal{Z}_k(I)\subseteq \mathbb{A}^d\) be a subvariety of \(\mathbb{A}^d\text{.}\) The coordinate ring of \(X\) is the ring \(k[X] := k[x_1,\dots,x_d]/\mathcal{I}(X)\text{.}\)

    Definition 4.21. Affine Algebra.

    An affine \(k\)-algebra is any ring of the form
    \begin{equation*} k[x_1,\ldots, x_n]/I \textrm{ for some ideal } I\subseteq k[x_1,\ldots, x_n]. \end{equation*}

    Definition 4.22. Pullback.

    Let \(k\) be a field. Let \(X\subseteq \A^m_k\) and \(Y \subseteq \A^n_k\) be affine varieties. Let \(\phi: X \to Y\) be a morphism given by \((g_1(x),\dots,g_n(x))\text{,}\) with \(g_i \in k[x_1,\dots,x_m]\text{.}\) We define
    \begin{equation*} \begin{CD} k[Y]@>\phi^*>> k[X]\\\\ f(y_{1},\dots,y_{n})@>>> f(g_{1}(x),\dots,g_n(x)) \end{CD} \end{equation*}

    Example 4.23. Exercise -.

    Show that the rule \(\phi^*\) is a well-defined ring homomorphism, and that the map \(\phi \mapsto \phi^*\) is well-defined.

    Example 4.24. Exercise -.

    For any field \(k\text{,}\) there is a contravariant functor from affine varieties over \(k\) to affine \(k\)-algebras that (a) on objects, maps a variety \(X\) to its coordinate ring \(k[X]\text{,}\) (b) on morphisms, maps a morphism of varieties \(X \xrightarrow{\phi} Y\) to its pullback \(k[Y] \xrightarrow{\phi^*} k[X]\text{.}\)

    Example 4.25. .

    We saw before that
    \begin{equation*} X= \{ (t,t^2,t^3) \ | \ t \in k \} \end{equation*}
    is a subvariety of \(\mathbb{A}^3_k\text{.}\) Thus its coordinate ring is of the form \(k[X] = k[x,y,z]/\cI(X)\text{.}\) To compute \(\cI(X)\text{,}\) note that the polynomials \(f \in k[x,y,z]\) that vanish at every point of \(X\) are precisely the polynomials in the kernel of the map \(k[x,y,z] \to k[t]\) given by \(x \mapsto t\text{,}\) \(y \mapsto t^2\text{,}\) and \(z \mapsto t^3\text{.}\) Then one can show that \(\cI(X) = (x^2-y,x^3-z)\text{.}\) For an explicit field like \(\mathbb{R}\) or \(\mathbb{C}\text{,}\) we can also compute this kernel in Macaulay2. Notice, in fact, that \(k[X] \cong k[t,t^2,t^3]\text{.}\)

    Example 4.26. .

    Fix a field \(k\text{,}\) and let \(R = k[x,y,z]\text{.}\) Consider the ideal \(P\) given by
    \begin{equation*} P = \left( \underset{f}{\underbrace{x^3-yz}}, \underset{g}{\underbrace{y^2-xz}}, \underset{h}{\underbrace{z^2-x^2y}} \right). \end{equation*}
    We will show that the ring homomorphism #empty is an isomorphism. First, note that it is immediately surjective by construction, so we just need to prove it is injective. If we set \(\deg(x)=3, \deg(y)=4, \deg(z)=5, \deg(t)=1\text{,}\) \(\pi\) is a graded homomorphism of graded rings, whose kernel is homogeneous. Since \([ k[t^3,t^4,t^5] ]_n\) is a \(1\)-dimensional vector space generated by \(t^n\) for all \(n\geqslant 3\) (and zero in degrees 1 and 2), it suffices to show that \(\dim( [\frac{k[x,y,z]}{P}]_n ) = 1\) for all \(n \geqslant 3\) (and zero in degrees 1 and 2).
    Given any monomial in \(\frac{k[x,y,z]}{P}\text{,}\) we can use the relations \(y^2-xz\text{,}\) \(z^2-x^2y\text{,}\) and \(yz-x^3\) to obtain an equivalent monomial where the sum of the \(y\) and \(z\) exponents is smaller until we get a monomial of the form \(x^a\text{,}\) \(x^a y\text{,}\) or \(x^a z\text{.}\) If \(n=1,2\text{,}\) there is no such monomial; if \(n\geqslant 3\text{,}\) there is exactly one, namely,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x^{n/3} & \text{if} \ n\equiv 0 \mod 3 \\ x^{(n-4)/3} y & \text{if} \ n\equiv 1 \mod 3 \\ x^{(n-5)/3} y & \text{if} \ n\equiv 2 \mod 3 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    This shows that \(P\) is the kernel of the map #empty Since \(k[t^3,t^4,t^5] \subseteq k[t]\) is a domain, we conclude that \(P\) is a prime ideal. In fact, \(P\) is a homogeneous ideal with the grading we considered above: our generators \(f\text{,}\) \(g\text{,}\) and \(h\) are now homogeneous, with \(\deg(f) = 9\text{,}\) \(\deg(g) = 8\text{,}\) and \(\deg(h) = 10\text{.}\)
    We showed in () that \(\mathcal{Z}(P)\) is the variety
    \begin{equation*} X = \{ (t^3, t^4, t^5) \mid t \in k \}. \end{equation*}
    Thus we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{I}(X) = P = \left( x^3-yz, y^2-xz, z^2-x^2y \right), \end{equation*}
    and that \(k[t^3,t^4,t^5] \cong k[x,y,z]/P\) is the coordinate ring \(k[X]\text{.}\)
