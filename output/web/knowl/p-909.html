  1. The integers \(\mathbb{Z}\text{,}\) or any quotient of \(\mathbb{Z}\text{,}\) which we write compactly as \(\mathbb{Z}/n\text{.}\)
  2. A polynomial ring, by which we typically mean \(R = k [x_1, \ldots, x_n]\text{,}\) a polynomial ring in finitely many variables over a field \(k\text{.}\)
  3. A quotient of a polynomial ring by an ideal \(I\text{,}\) say \(R = k[x_1, \ldots, x_n]/I\text{.}\)
  4. Rings of polynomials in infinitely many variables, \(R = k[x_1, x_2, \ldots]\text{.}\)
  5. Power series rings \(R = k[[ x_1, \ldots, x_n ]]\) over a field \(k\text{.}\) The elements are (formal) power series \(\displaystyle\sum_{a_i \geqslant 0} c_{a_1, \ldots, a_n} x_1^{a_1} \cdots x_n^{a_n}\text{.}\)
  6. While any field \(k\) is a ring, we will see that fields on their own are not very exciting from the perspective of the kinds of things we will be discussing in this class.
