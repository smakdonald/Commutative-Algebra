Example 4.19. \(\ex\) -.

  1. Let \(k\) be an infinite field. Consider \(X = \cZ(xy -1) \subseteq \A^2_k\) (i.e., \(X\) is a hyperbola) and define \(\phi: X \to \A^1_k\) by \(\phi(a,b) = a\text{.}\) Then \(\phi\) is an algebraic map (indeed, it is given by a linear polynomial) and its image is \(\A^1_k \setminus \{0\}\text{,}\) which is {} an algebraic subset of \(\A^1_k\text{.}\) So the set-theoretic image of a morphism of varieties need not be a variety.
  2. Take an infinite field, and let \(Y\) be the classical cuspidal curve:
    \begin{equation*} Y = \cZ(y^2 - x^3) \subseteq \A^2_k. \end{equation*}
    Define
    \begin{equation*} \phi: \A^1_K \to Y \quad \phi(t) = (t^2, t^3). \end{equation*}
This \(\phi\) is an algebraic map from \(\A^1_K\) to \(Y\text{,}\) since the component functions are polynomial functions of \(t\) and \((t^3)^2 - (t^2)^3 = 0\) for all \(t\text{.}\)
Note that this \(\phi\) is a bijection of sets. However, it is not an isomorphism. Indeed, if it were, we would have some map \(\psi\) such that \(\psi\circ \phi = \mathrm{id}_{\A^1_K}\text{.}\) This \(\psi\) would be given by a polynomial \(h\) in two variables such that \(h(t^2,t^3) = t\text{.}\) It is easy to see that no such \(h\) exists. (c) Consider \(\A^6_k\) as the space of \(2 \times 3\) matrices over \(K\) with coordinates \(x_{11},\dots,x_{23}\text{,}\) and consider \(\A^5_K\) as the space of pairs of \(2 \times 1\) and \(1\times 3\) matrices over \(K\) with coordinates \(y_1,y_2, z_1,z_2,z_3\text{.}\) The map of matrix multiplication from \(\A^5_k\) to \(\A^6_k\) is a regular map:
\begin{equation*} (y_1,y_2,z_1,z_2,z_3) \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} y_1 z_1 & y_1 z_2 & y_1 z_3 \\ y_2 z_1 & y_2 z_2 & y_2 z_3 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
